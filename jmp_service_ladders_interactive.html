Definition

Drinking water from an improved water source that is located on premises, available when needed, and free from fecal and priority chemical contamination.

What This Looks Like in a Real Community

Example: Sarah's Home in Nairobi, Kenya

Sarah has a piped water connection directly into her home. She turns on her kitchen tap and clean, treated water flows immediately. The water is tested regularly by the municipal water authority for microbial and chemical contamination. Her family doesn't worry about collecting water or whether it's safe to drink.

Key Criteria

  • Improved water source (piped, borehole, protected well)
  • Located on premises (no travel time required)
  • Available when needed (no shortages or interruptions)
  • Free from contamination (E. coli = 0 CFU/100mL)

Definition

Drinking water from an improved source for which collection time exceeds 30 minutes for a round trip, including queuing.

What This Looks Like in a Real Community

Example: Fatima's Household in Remote Ethiopia

Fatima walks 45 minutes each way to reach a protected well. With queuing time during busy hours, the total trip can take 2 hours. She can only make one or two trips per day, which limits how much water her family can use. The well is improved and protected, but the distance makes it difficult to collect enough water for all household needs.

Key Criteria

  • Improved water source (protected well, distant borehole)
  • Round trip collection time >30 minutes (including queuing)
  • Limits quantity of water that can be collected
  • Time burden affects women and children disproportionately

Definition

Drinking water from an unprotected dug well or unprotected spring.

What This Looks Like in a Real Community

Example: Maria's Village in Rural Nicaragua

Maria collects water from an unprotected spring where runoff from surrounding land can contaminate the water. The spring has no cover or protection, and animals sometimes drink from it. During the rainy season, the water becomes very muddy. Maria's children frequently suffer from diarrheal diseases.

Key Criteria

  • Unprotected dug well (no lining, cover, or pump)
  • Unprotected spring (no structure to prevent contamination)
  • High risk of contamination from surface runoff
  • Vulnerable to animal contact and environmental pollution

Definition

Drinking water directly from a river, dam, lake, pond, stream, canal, or irrigation canal.

What This Looks Like in a Real Community

Example: John's Settlement in South Sudan

John and his family collect water directly from a nearby river. They dip their containers into the same water where people wash clothes, animals drink, and upstream communities dispose of waste. The water is visibly brown during certain times of year. Waterborne diseases are common in his community, and boiling water is difficult due to lack of fuel.

Key Criteria

  • Direct collection from rivers, streams, or ponds
  • No treatment or protection
  • Highest contamination risk
  • Often shared with animals and used for multiple purposes
  • Associated with highest burden of waterborne disease

Definition

Use of improved facilities that are not shared with other households and where excreta are safely disposed of in situ or transported and treated offsite.

What This Looks Like in a Real Community

Example: Pedro's Home in Lima, Peru

Pedro's household has a flush toilet connected to a municipal sewer system. Wastewater flows through pipes to a treatment plant where it's processed before being safely released. His family uses the toilet with privacy and dignity, and there's no risk of contaminating the local environment or water sources.

Key Criteria

  • Improved facility (flush toilet, pit latrine with slab, composting toilet)
  • Not shared with other households (private use)
  • Excreta safely managed through sewer connection OR
  • Safely emptied and transported to treatment facility OR
  • Safely treated in situ (e.g., septic system)

What This Looks Like in a Real Community

Definition

Disposal of human feces in fields, forests, bushes, open bodies of water, beaches, or other open spaces, or with solid waste.

What This Looks Like in a Real Community

Example: Remote Village in Niger

Ibrahim's community has no latrines. Each morning before dawn, people walk to designated areas in nearby fields to defecate. Women and girls feel particularly unsafe during these early morning trips. During the rainy season, waste is washed into water sources. Children playing in the village are exposed to fecal contamination, leading to frequent illness.

Key Criteria

  • No use of any toilet or latrine facility
  • Defecation in open fields, forests, or bushes
  • Direct contamination of environment
  • Severe dignity and safety concerns, especially for women and girls
  • High disease transmission risk
  • SDG Target 6.2 aims to end open defecation by 2030

Definition

Availability of a handwashing facility on premises with soap and water.

What This Looks Like in a Real Community

Example: Rosa's Home in Guatemala

Rosa has a simple handwashing station right outside her latrine - a bucket with a tap (or a tippy-tap made from a plastic bottle), a bar of soap on a dish, and water that she refills daily. After using the toilet and before preparing food, family members wash their hands with soap and water. The setup is basic but effective at helping her family maintain good hygiene.

Key Criteria

  • Handwashing facility present on premises
  • Both soap AND water available
  • Can be simple (tippy-tap, bucket with tap) or permanent (sink with running water)
  • Located conveniently near toilet or food preparation area
  • Enables handwashing at critical times

What This Looks Like in a Real Community

Example: James' Household in Rural Malawi

James has a handwashing station (a bucket with a tap) near his latrine, but often the water container is empty because water is scarce and collected from a distant source. Sometimes soap runs out and isn't replaced for weeks because it's expensive. The facility exists, but without both soap and water consistently available, family members can't always wash their hands properly.

Key Criteria

  • Handwashing facility present
  • Missing soap OR missing water (or both)
  • May have water but no soap
  • May have soap but no water
  • Infrastructure exists but not functional for effective handwashing

Definition

No handwashing facility on premises.

What This Looks Like in a Real Community

Example: Refugee Settlement in Uganda

Esther and her family live in a temporary shelter with no designated handwashing facility. They might occasionally wash hands using a cup of water when available, but there's no specific place or system for handwashing. Soap is a luxury item. After using the communal latrine or before eating, thorough handwashing simply isn't possible, putting the family at high risk of disease transmission.

Key Criteria

  • No handwashing facility visible on premises
  • No designated place for handwashing
  • Hand hygiene practices likely very limited
  • High risk of disease transmission
  • Common in emergency settings, informal settlements, extremely poor households
